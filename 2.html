Ziggurat

The Ziggurat was located in Ur, Mesopotamia, which is currently known as Iraq. It was built in the twenty-first century BC, known as the bronze age. It was built by the Sumerians who covered both the interior and exterior in mud bricks and baked bricks, with an average base of either one-hundred and seventy feet by one-hundred and seventy feet or one-hundred twenty-five by one-hundred seventy. The ziggurats were used for rituals like animal sacrifices for a patron god, storage of woven fabrics, grains, and gems as offerings to their god, managing irrigation systems, and an armory. Priests would perform rituals and manage the irrigation ditch that ran through the ziggurat. This being said the structure's most prominent purpose was for the user(s) to be able to connect with their god. Some of the ziggurat remains and is open for tourists to visit.
